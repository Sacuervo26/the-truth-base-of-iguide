KERMIT - Backsplit Properties
Introduction
The purpose of this KERMIT is to provide a quick review of Backsplit properties. Because these
homes appear infrequently in our tasks, this refresher ensures the whole team maintains our high
standards.

Identify a Backsplit Property
Backsplit properties are characterized by staggered interior levels. Unlike traditional homes, the
floors are not stacked directly on top of one another. Instead, the home "steps" up or down
(please see image below).

Figure # 1 – Backsplit Layout

Another distinctive feature of backsplit homes is their stairs. Regular properties usually have
overlapping stairs on intermediate floors. In other words, the stairs up and the stairs down, that
start from the same floor, may be one on top of the other. Nevertheless, Backsplits have short
flights of stairs (4 to 6 steps) built in separate locations. Please refer to the image below showing
the stairs from the Main Floor in a Backsplit house; notice that the stairs are side by side and do
not overlap.

Another clue that may help you identify Backsplit properties is that the stairs are located in the
periphery of the floor. If the operator placed each level separately, nothing should be drafted after
the stairs.

Backsplit Specific Rules
There are three main rules that drafters must take into account to properly process a backsplit
home:

Stairs Length
As mentioned earlier, stairs are located at the periphery of the floor when the operator places
each level separately. With this configuration, nothing gets drafted past the stairs. Consequently,
each staircase can and should be drawn to full extension (from the first to the last step).
If every staircase is drawn fully, the same stairs, drawn from different floors, should match in
length. For example, the Stairs UP from the Main Floor to the 2nd Floor should have the same
length as the Stairs DN from the 2nd Floor to the Main Floor.

Stairs UP (Duplicated/Backsplit)
All Stairs Up in a backsplit home will be labelled “Stairs UP (duplicated/backsplit)” except for the
lowest floor. The lowest floor uses the standard "Stairs UP" label.

Figure # 5 – Stairs UP (Duplicated/Backsplit) Label

Stairs DN do not have any additional rules for backsplit homes. These stairs can be labelled as
usual on every floor.

Marking Interior Walls
Intermediate floors (not the top or bottom) will have walls that are shared between more than
one level. These walls need to be marked as interior. To do so, in “E-Mode,” select one or multiple
walls, right-click to access the menu and select the option “Mark Interior Wall (excluded from
perimeter)”.

Additional Observations
Should drafters separate/join floors?
Properties should always be drafted with the scan distribution submitted by the operator. If the operator
submits two levels together, they must be drafted as is. Drafters should not separate or join floors together.
WARNING:
The rule above has two exceptions in which drafters are allowed to move scans to a separate floor:
1. Landings, or Foyers, in ranch-style homes (where stairs up or down are the next immediate area)
need to be combined with a different level unless there’s significant overlap between floors.
2. If two levels in a backsplit home are submitted together, but there is an overlap of two areas that
can be drafted, the levels may be put on different floors.
