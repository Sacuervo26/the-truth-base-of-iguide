Room Label and Dimension Errors & Omissions
A room label error is when the space has been called something other than what it truly is.
●

(Eg. A space contains a sink and washer/dryer).
○

This space has been labeled as “Mudroom” by the Drafter.

○

The space is clearly not a “Mudroom” and the space should truly be called
“Laundry”).

A dimension error is when dimensions have been turned on for a room that hides them by
default such as closets and foyers, dimension arrows that have not been hidden where
appropriate such as storage under stairs, or the cross arrows have not been moved to the
furthest extents of the space.
Spaces must be:
●

Labeled according to the use (with some minor exceptions) or,

●

Labeled according to the scan names in the Project Explorer.

●

Dimensioned to the longest width and length.

●

Dimensioned to where the wall meets the floor not to the restricted height area.

●

Dimensioned as close as possible to corners without overlapping the walls.

Room Label Error Tolerances
When there is no indication of labels within the Project Explorer, then it is up to the Drafter to
make their best determination of labeling the space.
Please note that for Standard iGUIDEs some bedrooms may have office equipment in them
and the Project Explorer does not indicate a name, the space should be labeled as a bedroom
so long as it fits the criteria of a bedroom. For a Standard iGUIDE, all the following conditions
must be true for a room to be called bedroom:
●

must contain a wardrobe or closet

●

must contain a window.

●

The entry door should be solid and not see-through.

●

Room area should be at least 6.5 m2 (70sqft)

When a room/space does not satisfy all these conditions, then it should be named Office. If
there is a second room that does not fulfill the bedroom conditions, it should be named Den.
The Den should be the smallest room on the floor.
1. Good Example Notes:
a. L1 – 5PC from 4PC at scan 010
b. L1 – Incorrect label. Ensuite from Bath at 0023
c. L1 – Bedroom from Office at scan 0009
2. Bad Example Notes:
a. Wrong label.
b. Missing label.
c. Swapped labels around.

Dimensional Error Tolerances
Dimensions must be set to the longest width and longest length however this can be tricky in
odd shaped rooms. For odd shaped rooms where you’re struggling with the dimension arrows
to be perfectly placed in a corner, QA and Drafters will fit them as best as possible and will not
attribute them as an error unless they are nowhere near the full extents of the room.
Other than odd shaped rooms, there is no error tolerance on dimensions. They must be
displayed in rooms that display them. They must be removed from Storage under stairs.
1. Good Example Notes:
a. L1 – Incorrect dimensions Primary 0010
b. L1 – Delete dims under storage 009

Divisional Error Tolerances
Divisions have an error tolerance of 5cm/2”. This means that if you have to adjust the
boundary line of a space by 5cm/2”, you do not have to attribute it as an error towards the
Drafter. Simply correct the division and move on. If you have to adjust the division by anything
greater than 5cm/2” then you must attribute a level 1 error towards the Drafter. Note down
the error on the notepad in Draft and increase the tally by 1 per instance.
Be careful when it comes to preference when spaces are not clearly divided. Use wall corners
and natural divisions to make your best judgment. Deciding to increase the size of a space
when divisions are unclear is preference on your end as the QA not an error on the Drafter.
1. Good Example Notes:
a. L1 - D3 not drafted to floor material change near Kitchen and Dining. Moved 3”
b. L1 - Missed division for Dining area near 008. Check ceiling for hanging lights to
indicate dining area.
2. Bad Example Notes:
a. Adjusted boundary.
b. Redivided the rooms.
c. Moved the invisible divider.
Take a look at how much detail is provided for the “Good Example”. The location of the
problem/issue should be explained using the floor it is on, the pano/scans that can see it and
what room it’s contained in or next to. We must be descriptive by describing the issue and if
applicable, provide a solution or tips as to how to avoid it in future. The “Bad Example”
provides no details for the Drafter and is completely useless. Providing “Bad Example” notes
will not be tolerated.
