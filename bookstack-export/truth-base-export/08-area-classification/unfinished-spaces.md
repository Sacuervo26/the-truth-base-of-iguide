Unfinished Spaces
Understanding Unfinished Spaces
ANSI Z765-20XX defines Finished Area as, “An enclosed area in a house that is suitable for year
round use based upon its location, embodying walls, floors, and ceilings that are similar to the
rest of the house.”
Walls would have to be drywalled, paneled, tiled, plastered, etc. to be considered finished. Bare
concrete (untreated) or stud framing is considered unfinished.
Floors would have to have carpeting, hardwood floors, laminate floors, epoxied concrete
floors, etc. to be considered finished. Bare concrete floors (untreated) or exposed subfloor
(plywood sheathing) is considered unfinished.
Ceilings would have to be drywalled, plastered or have acoustic paneling to be considered
finished. Exposed roof rafters, roof joists, floor joists, utility/plumbing pipes/vents are
considered unfinished.
Eg. A laundry room that has exposed ceilings and exposed/untreated concrete floors is
considered unfinished, while a laundry room with finished walls (drywalled), ceiling (drywalled),
and stamped concrete flooring is considered finished.
Does the space have finished ceilings?
Does the space have finished floors?
Does the space have finished walls?
If you answered yes to all 3 questions, the space will be treated as finished.
If you answered no to any of the 3 questions, the space will be treated as unfinished.
Note: This is in addition to checking if the space should be included or excluded based on
heating and in addition to checking if the space should be included or excluded based on
restricted height.

Unfinished Stairs
When determining if stairs should be labeled as unfinished it is important to reference the
surrounding areas on the same floor.
Stairs down will be flagged as included-unfinished when the stairs, access area preceding the
stairs and any landings have unfinished attributes. If only some of these areas are unfinished
the stairs will not be unfinished. For example, a set of stairs down is unfinished, but the
hallway in which the stairs are accessed are finished, then the stairs will be flagged as finished.

Stairs up will be flagged as included-unfinished when access to the area under the stairs is
unfinished. Typically the storage area under stairs is a space that is lacking traditional

In other words:
Use STAIRS DN (unfinished) only if stairs, landing and adjacent area that gives access to it are
all unfinished.
Use STAIRS UP (unfinished) only if the area under the stairs and the room connected to the
area under stairs are unfinished.
Storage underneath stairs will not be removed or excluded from the square footage of the
floor based on restricted height unless the entire floor does not meet a ceiling height clearance
of 7ft or more (allowable error tolerance to leave included is 6’-11”).

Labeling
It is important to try to utilize standard label options given by Draft when labeling a property.
For example, when labeling a storage area, Draft will offer two options: “Storage” and “Storage
(unfinished).” By selecting the appropriate label offered by Draft, potential room type and area
flag errors can be avoided as Draft will automatically and correctly set the necessary type and
flag.

In situations where a room is unfinished, but Draft does not offer an “(unfinished)” option

Figure 5 - Area flag set for a room that Draft does not offer an (unfinished) option
When areas that are unfinished have been labeled correctly and the area flags have been
correctly set, upon first glance, the drafted floor plan will not look any different than if all
rooms were to have been flagged as “Included-Finished”. For this reason, it is crucial that
drafters utilize the Inspect Area feature within Draft to determine which rooms have had their
area flags modified.
To check for Included-Unfinished area flags, start by toggling the Inspect Area button on the
toolbar. This will show all included areas on the floor plan to appear as yellow, excluded areas
will appear blue, and exterior areas will appear gray. Upon holding the “Shift” key, any
“Included-Unfinished” areas will change from appearing yellow to appearing blue.
