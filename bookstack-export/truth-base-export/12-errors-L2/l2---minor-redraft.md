Minor Re-Drafting Work
Minor re-drafting work (resulting in changes to reported SF that is less than 2%).
Minor redrafting work is a slight modification to how lines or walls are drawn. When the
alignment is correct but something did not get drafted correctly, it’ll require a correction that
may only affect the square footage of the internal space, not the property overall. Eg. a wall
has been drafted on a curve but doesn’t exactly fit the laser data. This wall is then redrafted to
more accurately align with the laser data.
Another example of minor re-drafting work is when exterior walls are not drafted to laser and
need to be thickened to match exterior laser data.
Redrafting work that is very common is for curved stairs. Oftentimes a drafter will draft curved
stairs too wide or too thin or even stop too short. It is up to the QA to recognize this and redraft it if the lines cannot be easily manipulated.
Other minor redrafting work that may affect the total reported square footage by less than 2%
is when:
●

An exterior wall was not drawn close enough to the provided laser data.

●

An exterior wall is actually proven to be on an angle and the Drafter drew the wall
straight.

Minor Redrafting Error Tolerances
There is no tolerance for any kind of redrafting work. While the determination between minor
and major redrafting work is based on a 2% square footage change, QA are not expected to
make this calculation and the classification on whether it is an L2 for minor or L3 for major is
based on the QA’s experience. In situations where a drafter may disagree if the error is L2 for
minor or L3 for major, a shift lead can investigate and pass judgement on the error in
question.
1. Good Example Notes:
a. L2 – minor redraft of laundry at 017. Wall not drafted to laser.
b. L2 – minor redraft of bedroom at 0005-0007. Angled walls per laser data.
*Note: The importance of being descriptive here is to inform the drafter WHY it had to be
redrafted.
Bad Example Notes:
c. Redrafted bedroom.
d. Redrafted laundry.
Take a look at how much detail is provided for the “Good Example”. The location of the
problem/issue should be explained using the floor it is on, the pano/scans that can see it and
what room it’s contained in or next to. We must be descriptive by describing the issue and if
applicable, provide a solution or tips as to how to avoid it in future. The “Bad Example”
provides no details for the Drafter and is completely useless. Providing “Bad Example” notes
will not be tolerated.
*Note: The intent of the error tolerance is to ensure we are not overloading every Drafter with
several minuscule errors. This does not mean we do not correct the error in question. We
always correct everything whether it is within or outside of the error tolerance. The error
tolerance in this document is to be strictly adhered to, making up your own standards or
tolerances will not be permitted.
