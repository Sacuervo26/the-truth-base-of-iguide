Windows
A Window is an opening in a wall, door, roof, or vehicle that allows for the exchange of light
and may also allow for the passage of sounds and sometimes air.

Press “W” to open the window placement mode.
Use the spacebar to move between every room and inspect scans for any windows.
Windows will automatically snap to any wall.
Adjusting the window size is as follows:
o Press “Shift + Mouse wheel” to adjust the length of the window by 2”
increments (1” increase on either end of the window object).
o Utilize the yellow arrows on either end of the window segment to manually
adjust the window by 1” increments.
o With a window object selected, press the “-” or “+” button to increase or
decrease the window object by 1” increments (½” on either side).
Making sure the window object is sized from Inside of the frame to inside of the
frame.
Ensure the window width is as accurate as possible to the nearest inch using the two
dashed yellow lines on the Pano Stripe Viewer.
If the window edge is not visible or hidden behind a curtain use best judgment.
o Can often utilize natural light to determine the potential extents.
o Typically, window sizes are common from room to room (bedroom to
bedroom).
Ignore roof windows, roof lights or skylights as we draft the exterior wall to where it
meets with the floor so those windows (if put in) are misrepresented.

Windows in Draft Software does not support the indication of mullions, the window when
placed within a wall, will be used as if it did not contain any mullions. Operators may request
that windows be split based on the mullions for certain drafts but this is not done by default.

Fixed
A fixed window type is set in the closed position at all times. It is stationary and cannot open
(non-operational). These kinds of windows often look like “picture frame” windows but they
tend to have larger and thicker frames.
This window type is most often used in living rooms, dining rooms, family rooms, bathrooms,
basements, nurseries, tall hallways, and rooms with vaulted ceilings.

Single-Hung/Double Hung
A single-hung window (also known as a single-sash window) is a type of window with a
lower, operable sash and an upper fixed sash. This indicates that the bottom portion of the
window will slide up and down while the upper portion remains fixed/stationary. This type of
window provides ventilation through the bottom sash.
A double-hung window (also known as a double-sash window) is a type of window that has
two operable sashes that can both slide up and down. This type of window can provide
optimal ventilation and is common in two-story homes.

Single-hung windows are often used on the lowest floors to bring in cool air, while doublehung ones are used on the upper floors to let the rising hot air escape out the upper sash.
Hung windows are used most often in homes with classic traditional styling, though they are
also found in traditional-modern homes. The classic rambler, farmhouse, and bungalow
styles, for example, make extensive use of these windows.

Casement
A casement window (also called a crank window) is designed using hinges (attached to its
frame by one or more hinges at the side) that allow the window to open at the side of the
frame. Typical casement windows are operable by the use of a crank through an interior
metal handle. They can be installed as a singular entity or in pairs within a common frame.

Awning
An awning style window (also called a crank window) is similar to a casement window but
these types of windows are hinged at the top and open outward from the bottom. This
window is designed so the bottom of the sash will swing outward from the home when
opened.These are often used on higher walls or in combination with larger fixed window
types. These windows are more commonly wider than they are tall.

Hopper
A hopper style window is a smaller, narrow window that opens from the top, essentially the
reverse of an awning style window. This window style is designed so that the top of the sash
will swing inward from the wall when opened.

Sliding
A sliding window type (also called gliding windows) have sashes that will slide (or glide) left
or right in either direction contained within a single frame. These are operable windows that
open by sliding the sash horizontally a;og the bottom portion of the window (the sill).
Sliding windows can come in a variety of styles — Single Sliders, Double Sliders, and Triple
Sliders (which consist of two operable sashes and one stationary sash).

Jalousie
A jalousie type window (also called a louvered window) is a window consisting of several
parallel glass, acrylic or wooden louvers set within a frame. The louvers are joined onto a
track so that they can tilt open or tilt shut in unison.

Anatomy of a Window

Figure #30 - Window Anatomy Provided by Marvin

Glazing
Glazing refers to the panes of glass set within the sashes of a window. Glazing in more
modern homes/properties are composed of at least two layers (panes) of glass and
sometimes three. Inert gas, commonly argon, is injected between the glass layers to provide
additional insulation.

Daylight Opening (DLO)
This is the area of visible glass in a glazed sash. It’s the total amount of glass in a window not
including the sash stiles and rails or the window frame.

19

Sash
This is a portion of the window that holds the glass. Individual sashes are commonly paned
windows but could also contain a single sheet of glass.

Stiles
Stiles are the main vertical supports for the frame of the window sash.

Rails
Rails are the main horizontal supports of the sash. For double-hung windows, the check rail
is the part where the bottom portion of an upper sash and the upper portion of the lower
sash come into contact with each other.

Storm Sash
This is an outside component to protect the integrity of the window against severe weather
conditions.

Figure #31 - Window Anatomy Provided by Marvin

20

Head Jamb
The head jamb is the topmost, horizontal component forming the top of the window frame.

Side Jamb
The side jambs are the main vertical components forming the sides of a window frame.

Sill
The sill is the bottom most horizontal component forming the window frame. The window sill
is meant to structurally support and hold in place the window. The external, outside portion
of a window sill is a sloped component for shedding rainwater away from the wall.

Operating Handle
Also known as the crank, found on casement windows, it is the handle or device that is used
to open and/or close the sash.

Operating Arm
Attached to both the window frame and the sash on casement, awning, and hopper
windows. It is the mechanism that works in conjunction with an operating handle to open and
close a sash. The operating arm will also hold a sash in position when opened.

Locking Handle
The locking handle is meant to pull the two sashes tight together for security as well as
energy efficiency.

Muntin Bars (Grilles)
The muntin bar is a divider between the window panes. These are located on the window
glass itself, turning the glass into a grid. Also referred to as grilles, these are strips of material
that simulate smaller panes of glass, which often form a grid.

Casing
Window casings are the moldings/trim that wrap around the window frames. Installed on the
outside of the home, they’re meant to seal the window frame to the house, blocking cold air
from entering the interior. Installed on the inside of the home, casings are the finishing trim to
a window installation (similar to baseboards or door moldings).

21

Screens
A window screen (also referred to as an insect screen, bug screen, window net) is designed
to cover the opening of a window. It is commonly made of metal, fiberglass or plastic wire. It
acts as a barrier to keep debris from entering the home while allowing for outside air to flow
in.

Figure #32 - Window Anatomy Provided by Marvin

Triple Pane
A triple pane window, also referred to as a triple-glazed window, has three panes of glass.
There is a gap in between the panes of glass, typically filled with an insulating gas like argon.

22

Cladding
Cladding for windows is a vinyl or metal covering that covers a wooden core. This is found on
the outside of the home, exterior side of the window. Cladding helps preserve the wood and
improves the weather resistance of the assembly. Cladding is the application where a
material will act as a skin or layer of protection over another material.

Mullion
Window mullions are singular, vertical members that separate two sides of a singular
window.

Figure #33 - Window Anatomy Provided by Marvin

Window Drip Cap
A drip cap is an “L” shaped flashing that is added over doors and windows after they’ve been
installed to prevent any moisture from entering the home from above. This is typically
installed before the siding is installed.

23

Nailing Fin
Nailing fins, sometimes called mounting flanges, are the thin strips installed on the exterior
sides of a window.

Rough Opening
A rough opening is the space cut out of a wall where a window or door will be installed.
